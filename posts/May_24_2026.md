# Leveraging AI to scale our ability to form mechanistic hypotheses

I recently joined an AIxScience builder community run by @Worldwide Studios. The idea: spend 100 days actively building and ship demoable releases every two weeks.  I've been trying to figure out a theme for this 100 days and have settled on "JARVIS for drug discovery".  

JARVIS from the Iron Man movies is my North Star for benevolent AI. If you don't know what I'm talking about, **[watch this scene](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ddk9ci6geSs&t=35s)**. It's an amazing depiction of of how AI can unleash scientific creativity.  Building that is what I want to spend my time doing personally and professionally.  

So I'm building with JARVIS in mind, and using this POV to think about hard problems. In a nutshell, I think **JARVIS for biology needs to be an agentic interface to three primary capabilities**:

**The ability to form and validate mechanistic hypotheses at scale.** Biology has exabyte-scale data (petabyte-scale after dimensionality reduction) and no human can reason over it.  We need AI but agents are bound by token cost, and petabyte-scale data can be enormously expensive to reason over. What can we do about it?

**Once we can formulate hypotheses at scale, we need to experimentally test them at scale.** Here we hit human scaling limits. Most experiments are still run by humans, and humans don't scale. We need physical AI but most labs aren't set up for AI-driven experimentation, and most automation vendors aren't ready for it either. What can we do about this?

**As data accumulates, we need to speed information flow across silos.**  A team in Development might be building a therapeutic that decreases expression of a target because they believe it confers clinical benefit — while a human genetics scientist in Pre-Clinical Research has found evidence that runs counter to that hypothesis. How do we get that information to the right people before the wrong decisions get made?

That's the full vision. This sprint was about the first capability.

Here's what I delivered:

1.  A written analysis of the key challenges preventing AI from reasoning over biological data at scale. TL;DR: I think we need an inference-oriented architecture — and I wrote up what that means concretely.
2. A demo that shows a v0 prototype of this architecture running end to end ( [github](https://github.com/dorkosaurus/JARVIS_for_bio) | [Loom video]() ).

I'm building in public to expose my thinking on purpose. Would love feedback, thoughts, or recommendations. And if you're interested in partnering with me on this, just DM me.
